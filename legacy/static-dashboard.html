Product Analytics Case Study

The riskiest 20% of users account for over half of all churn — here's how to find them before they leave.

A streaming platform wanted a churn model it could actually act on, not just a KPI dashboard. This is how a behavioral risk score outperformed demographic segmentation, what the model gets wrong, and what I'd do next with more data.

01 — The Question

A quarter of users churn every cycle. Which quarter matters more than the average.

Base churn rate across the platform sits at 26.9%. Averages like that are useless operationally — a retention team can't target "everyone." The real question: can a small, well-targeted slice of the user base be identified before they leave, accurately enough to be worth acting on?

I modeled the problem across four linked tables — users, watch history, ratings, and content metadata — using SQL for aggregation and segmentation, Python (pandas + scikit-learn) for feature engineering and a churn model, and Power BI for the executive-facing views.

02 — What Doesn't Predict Churn (Much)

Demographics are close to noise. Behavior isn't.

Country, subscription tier, and age band all sit within a couple of points of the base rate — none of them are useful targeting criteria on their own:

Favorite genre shows a wider raw spread (Drama users churn at 30.8% vs. Thriller at 23.9%), but that gap mostly disappears in the multivariate model — genre coefficients shrink to near-zero once watch time and recency are controlled for. The likely explanation: genre preference is a weak proxy for how much someone actually watches, not a cause of churn in its own right. That distinction — a variable being predictive on its own but redundant once you control for the real driver — is a classic confounding pattern worth checking for before trusting any single-variable chart.

03 — What Actually Predicts Churn

Two behavioral signals, plus a support-ticket and tenure effect.

A logistic regression across all available features found days since last login and cumulative watch time dominating every other coefficient — by roughly 5-7x. Two secondary signals also mattered: users filing more support tickets churned more, and newer accounts churned far more than long-tenured ones.

04 — The Finding

Ranking beats thresholding. The top 20% of scored users capture 55% of all churn.

Rather than pick a single cutoff on any one metric, I scored every user with the trained model and sorted them into risk deciles. The result is a clean, monotonic lift curve — exactly what you want from a usable risk score, and a much more honest picture than a hard threshold would give:

Churn rate by predicted risk decile
Decile 1 = lowest 10% of predicted risk · Decile 10 = highest 10%

The top 2 deciles — 20% of the user base — contain 55% of everyone who actually churns. Targeting retention spend at that group instead of the full base cuts outreach volume by 5x while still reaching over half of at-risk users.

Recall sits at 50.9% at the model's default 0.5 threshold — meaning it misses about half of churners if used as a binary yes/no classifier. The decile/lift view sidesteps that limitation: instead of asking the model for a verdict, it asks for a ranking, and spends the retention budget top-down until the budget runs out. That reframing — from classification to ranking — is usually the difference between a churn model that sits in a slide deck and one a team actually uses.

05 — Recommendations

Spend the retention budget on rank, not on rules.

01

Route deciles 9-10 into a proactive win-back flow

That's 20% of users capturing 55% of churn — the single highest-leverage segment to spend retention budget on, rather than a blanket campaign.

02

Build a first-90-day onboarding track

Churn is 44% in the first 90 days vs. 18% after 2 years. This is a distinct problem from general disengagement and needs its own intervention — better onboarding, early content recommendations, milestone nudges.

03

Flag support-ticket spikes as a retention signal, not just a support metric

Users with 3+ tickets churn at 57% vs. 21% for zero tickets. Support and retention teams should share this signal instead of operating on separate systems.

06 — Methodology, Limitations & What I'd Do Next

Being honest about what this model can't tell you.

Data: a modeled dataset simulating a mid-sized subscription streaming service — 25,000 users, 100K+ watch sessions, 40K ratings, 500 titles across 10 countries — generated with a noisy, multi-factor process (not a hand-set rule) so the churn signal is realistic rather than a clean cliff. Favorite genre is derived from actual watch behavior per user, not assigned independently, and every watch session is constrained to fall between that user's signup date and last login — internal consistency a naive random generator wouldn't have.

Model: logistic regression (scikit-learn) on standardized features, trained/test split 75/25, stratified by churn. 0.863 AUC and 82.4% accuracy are strong but not suspiciously perfect — recall of 50.9% at the default threshold is a real limitation, not an oversight, and is exactly why the recommendation above is a ranked decile approach rather than a binary flag.

What I'd add with more data: payment/billing events (failed charges are usually a stronger churn signal than almost anything else and aren't in this dataset), customer service transcript sentiment, competitor pricing changes, and device/platform crash logs. I'd also want a longer observation window to build a true survival model instead of a single snapshot classifier.
